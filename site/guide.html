How to read this dashboard

This dashboard tracks a small set of macro and market conditions that showed up, in various combinations, before past financial crises — the 2000 dot-com bust, the 2007-08 financial crisis, 1987's Black Monday, and others back to 1973. The goal isn't to predict the next crash. It's to let you see, in plain numbers, whether today's economy and markets resemble the run-up to any of those past episodes, and which specific ingredient (cheap money, excess debt, extreme valuations, credit stress…) looks most like the historical pattern. Below is what each part of the dashboard shows, in the order it appears, and — just as important — what it does not mean.

1. Composite gauge & regime bands

The big dial at the top blends the five pillar scores below into one 0–100 "composite" number: a weighted average of how extreme today's key indicators are versus their own history back to 1975. It's a percentile-style reading, not a price level or a forecast. The four color bands — Cool, Warm, Frothy, Bubble risk — are historical quantiles of that same composite: the Cool/Warm line sits at the 50th percentile (64), Warm/Frothy at the 85th (76), and Frothy/Bubble-risk at the 95th (83). Because "Cool" is defined as below the historical median, half of all history counts as Cool by construction — a Warm or Frothy reading means conditions are stretched versus history, not that a crash is imminent. The "rolling 20y" toggle is a secondary, display-only view using just the last 20 years for recency context; every alert and the sequence tracker always use the full-history version underneath.

2. Pillar bars

The five bars are the composite's ingredients, weighted differently in the blend: Valuation (30%) — how expensive stocks are versus earnings and GDP; Leverage (25%) — how much debt is built up in the system (household and corporate debt, credit spreads, margin debt, bank lending standards); Liquidity (20%) — how much cheap money is available (money-supply growth, the Fed's balance sheet, real interest rates, the yield curve); Sentiment (15%) — how speculative markets are behaving (the VIX, Bitcoin); and Macro (10%) — market breadth, whether gains are broad or narrow. One hot pillar does not make a hot composite: through 1980-81, the leverage pillar sat at a then-record 77.5–86.1 (Frothy-to-Bubble-risk territory) on genuinely high household and corporate debt, yet the composite stayed Cool (37.8–54.6) throughout, because valuation was near its all-time cheapest (Shiller CAPE ≈9.65 in November 1980) and offset it. Read the bars alongside the gauge, not instead of it.

3. Confirmation stress gauge

Next to the gauge is a separate reading, "Confirmation stress." It averages four indicators: the Sahm rule (a recession-timing rule based on unemployment), the Chicago Fed's CFNAI activity index, whether the S&P 500 has broken below its 200-day average, and the trade-weighted dollar. These are deliberately kept out of the composite score above, because historically they only turn hot during or after a downturn is already under way — folding them into the "bubble" score made it rise during past crashes instead of warning ahead of them. Below 40 reads Quiet, 40–70 Elevated, above 70 Confirming. Think of the composite as "how stretched is the setup" and this gauge as "has the break actually started" — two different questions, answered by two different sets of indicators.

4. Pre-crisis sequence tracker

This tracker watches for six classic pre-bust conditions: (1) valuation stretched for a long stretch, (2) margin debt peaking then rolling over, (3) the yield curve re-steepening after having been inverted, (4) credit spreads widening off their lows, (5) market breadth diverging from the index near its highs, and (6) price confirming with a break below its 200-day average alongside recession or volatility signals. "Engaged" means at least two of stages 1–3 have fired, or — for credit-driven downturns like 1990 — stages 3 and 4 are both true at once. A stage "lapses" (stops counting) after its condition is false for about three months; the tracker resets once the market later falls 20% from its post-engagement high. One nuance worth remembering: stages needn't fire in numeric order, and "current stage" just means the highest-numbered stage fired right now — so "Engaged, stage 3" doesn't mean stages 1 and 2 already happened, it means the pattern is partly formed and stage 3's own signal (curve re-steepening) is the one lit up. Read the labeled signal, not just the number.

5. Score history chart

This chart plots the composite — and, via the tabs, each pillar plus the confirmation-stress gauge — back through history, with the Cool/Warm/Frothy/ Bubble-risk bands shaded behind it. Vertical lines mark past crisis peaks: red for the episodes with full narrative and data support (1973, 1980, 1987, 1990, 2000, 2007, 2020, 2022), gray for two older, thinner-data episodes shown only as markers (1929, 1937). A translucent S&P 500 line (log scale) rides along for price context. The most useful way to read it: compare today's composite level and shape to the run-up before each red line — was it climbing for years, like 1997–2000, or spiking suddenly, like early 2020? — rather than treating any single crossing of a band edge as a buy or sell signal by itself. Directly beneath the score sits a "Macro backdrop" strip on the same timeline (zooming or panning one row moves the other with it): CPI and the Fed funds rate are shown by default, with core CPI, PPI, payrolls growth, and unemployment one click away in its legend, so you can line up the score's moves against the inflation/growth/policy backdrop without leaving the chart. The gray vertical bands spanning both rows are official NBER recession dates — a government body's after-the-fact judgment call about when a recession happened, not anything this site calculates or predicts.

6. Closest historical analogs

This card finds which past episode's build-up today's indicator profile most resembles. Each past episode was "snapshotted" at fixed points before its peak — for example, "dotcom T−24" means the market's condition 24 months before the March 2000 dot-com peak. Today's percentile profile is compared against every snapshot by cosine similarity, after first subtracting the neutral 50th-percentile baseline from every value, so the comparison measures real agreement rather than the fact that two all-positive lists of numbers always look somewhat alike. A match above 0.98 is notable specifically because, across a 474-month backtest replay (1987–2026), only the top 10% of months' best matches reach that high — a typical month's best match is a much lower ~0.84. Most days should show no strong analog at all; that's the design working as intended.

7. Macro context panel

CPI, core CPI, PPI, payroll growth, unemployment, job openings, and the Fed funds rate appear here as tiles, and (except job openings) as the backdrop strip under the score history chart in section 5 — both are background information only. They help explain why conditions are what they are (is liquidity tight because the Fed is fighting inflation, or because growth is slowing?) but are deliberately excluded from every scored calculation on this page — the composite, the pillars, the confirmation gauge, the analog comparisons, and the sequence tracker all ignore them entirely. Their percentile chips are a plain historical rank of the raw value (higher number always means a higher percentile; none of these are "inverted" the way a couple of pillar indicators are). One gap worth knowing: the ISM manufacturing survey, a classic activity gauge, is no longer freely available, so the Chicago Fed's CFNAI (found in the confirmation-stress gauge, section 3) stands in as the activity proxy instead.

8. Indicator drill-down

Pick any single indicator — or up to four at once with "+ Compare" — to see its raw-value history and its percentile-versus-own-history ("froth") chart side by side, with dotted red lines marking the 80th and 90th percentile levels and an S&P 500 overlay for price context on the raw chart. With one indicator selected, this is exactly the single-indicator view. Comparing several works differently for the two charts, on purpose: percentiles all share one 0–100 axis, because "rank versus own history" is directly comparable across indicators no matter their units, while raw values each get their own separate mini-chart, because a debt ratio, an interest rate, and a stock index aren't measured in the same units and would be meaningless stacked on one shared axis. The raw and percentile views (and, in compare mode, every indicator's row) sit on a single shared timeline — zooming or panning any one of them moves all of them together.

9. Backtest & honesty

The Backtest page (linked in the nav) checks whether the sequence tracker would actually have caught past crises. Five criteria: would it have reached stage 4 or higher within the 18 months before each of four real peaks (dot-com 2000, the 2007-08 financial crisis, the 1990 recession, and the 2022 post-COVID unwind), and did it stay quiet — no false "engaged" reading — through all of 2019 as a control? Current score: 4 of 5 pass. The one honest failure is dot-com: the tracker never advances past stage 0 before that peak — the margin-debt series doesn't reach far enough back to qualify (a data-coverage gap), and the yield curve's re-steepening arrived only after the March 2000 peak, so a second early-warning stage was never available in time. The standing rule behind every number on this site: thresholds are only changed when new evidence is written down in advance, never adjusted after the fact just to turn a failing check green.

10. Data freshness & caveats

Every series updates automatically once a day through a scheduled job (with a same-day catch-up run as backup); each has a "staleness budget" — daily series are flagged if more than about a week old, monthly ones after about 45 days, quarterly after about 120 days, adjusted for each series' normal reporting lag. Some government series get revised after first release; the site keeps the latest revision, not a frozen first print. Historical percentiles are computed point-in-time — each date on the charts only reflects what would have been knowable as of that date, with no hindsight baked in. Standing caveat, worth repeating: this dashboard is a study tool for understanding how past crises built up and whether today rhymes with them. It is not investment advice, and no single reading here should be treated as a buy, sell, or timing signal.
