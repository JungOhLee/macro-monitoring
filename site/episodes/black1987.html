Black Monday (peak: August 1987)

Type: market-structure crash on a valuation/momentum extreme. The archetype of fast stress with no economic transmission - the episode that sizes what a trend rule can and cannot save.

The setup

Jan-Aug 1987 the S&P rose 39% on top of a five-year bull, fueled by takeover/LBO mania and a weak post-Plaza dollar. Rates rose against it all year: the 10Y went from ~7% in January to ~10% by mid-October, and the Fed hiked the discount rate Sept 4 - the first increase since 1984 - partly to defend the dollar under the February 1987 Louvre Accord. Portfolio insurance (dynamic hedging) covered an estimated $60-90bn of equities: a machine pre-committed to selling into declines.

The causal chain

Momentum extreme (trailing P/E ~22 against a 10Y that rose from 8.7% at the peak to 10% by mid-October) -> portfolio insurance concentrates a huge latent sell order -> triggers Oct 14-16: ugly August trade deficit, a House bill killing takeover-debt deductibility, then Baker's weekend threat to let the dollar fall against the D-mark -> the -9.1% week forces insurance selling at Monday's open -> index arbitrage breaks, futures trade at record discounts to cash and drag the cash market down -> -20.5% in one session -> Fed liquidity backstop -> no economic transmission, fast recovery.

The turn

The S&P peaked Aug 25 at 336.77. The week of Oct 12-16 fell 9.1%: the index first closed below its 200-DMA Thursday Oct 15 (marginally, at -11.5% from the peak) and broke decisively below it Friday Oct 16 at -16%. Monday Oct 19: -20.5%, the worst single day in US market history. The closing trough came Dec 4 at 223.92, -33.5% from the peak - nearly all of it inside eight weeks, most of it in four sessions. Greenspan's one-sentence liquidity pledge before the open Oct 20, plus Fed pressure on banks to keep funding dealers, stopped the run. No recession followed - 1988 real GDP grew ~4% and the S&P made a new high by July 1989.

What it looked like in the indicators

  • Valuation: CAPE ~18 - elevated for the era, far below 1929/2000. The sharper extreme was ERP: earnings yield ~5% against a 10Y at 10%, deteriorating all year as prices and yields rose together.
  • Liquidity: uniformly tightening - M2 YoY roughly halved from 1986's ~9%, nominal fed funds rising (~6.4% to ~7.3% by October), discount rate hiked Sept 4. But the 10Y-3M curve stayed steeply positive (~+300bp) - correctly signaling no recession.
  • Credit: Baa spreads narrowed from ~265bp to ~190bp into the peak and widened only ~50bp, briefly, after Oct 19; household/corporate debt-to-GDP showed no extreme. The credit pillar was right to stay quiet - this was not a credit event.
  • Trend/sentiment: advisory sentiment hit bull extremes into August; the S&P first closed below its 200-DMA Thursday Oct 15 - marginally, at -11.5% from the peak - and broke decisively Friday Oct 16 at -16%, one session before Black Monday.
  • Data limits: VIX (1990+; the backfilled VXO printed ~150 on Oct 19), SLOOS (1990+), this monitor's margin-debt series (1997+), and RSP/SPY breadth (2003+) all postdate the episode.

Lessons for the monitor

Nothing sequenced: credit, curve, Sahm, and CFNAI stayed benign because the economy was fine. Only the trend rule fired - Oct 15 at -11.5%, confirmed Oct 16 at -16% - into a -34% drawdown: enough to dampen the tail, not avoid it. With no macro transmission, re-entry mattered as much as exit: new highs came within two years.
