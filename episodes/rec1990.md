# 1990 credit-crunch bear (peak: July 1990)

**Type: credit-crunch recession bear with an exogenous oil kicker.** Proof that
a -20% bear needs no valuation excess - the credit and rates pillars did all
the work here, at moderate drawdown scale.

## The setup
The 1980s LBO boom left corporate debt/GDP at a then-record; the junk bond
market that financed it froze after Campeau and the UAL deal collapse (Oct 13,
1989 mini-crash, Dow -6.9%). The S&L system was in liquidation (FIRREA/RTC,
Aug 1989) and banks were eating commercial real estate losses into new Basel
capital rules. The Fed had tightened to ~9.75% by early 1989 against ~5%
inflation - real funds near 5% - then eased only slowly.

## The causal chain
1980s LBO + CRE credit boom -> S&L failures and bank capital erosion amplify ->
restrictive real rates and junk market collapse (Drexel bankruptcy Feb 13,
1990) trigger -> banks tighten lending, credit crunch chokes small-firm
activity -> recession begins July 1990 -> Iraq invades Kuwait Aug 2, oil
more than doubles ($17 -> ~$40), confidence collapses -> -20% equity decline.

## The turn
Credit broke nine months before equities (Oct 1989 junk freeze, Feb 1990
Drexel). The S&P peaked July 16, 1990 at 368.95 - the same month the NBER
recession began. The invasion hit two weeks later; the S&P bottomed October
11 at 295.46, -19.9% in under three months. The recession ended March 1991;
the index made new highs by February 1991. Short, sharp, credit-driven.

## What it looked like in the indicators
- Valuation pillar: silent, correctly. CAPE ~17-18 and Buffett indicator
  ~50-55% of GDP (the monitor's NCBEILQ027S/GDP series reads ~53% at 1990Q2) -
  near long-run averages. A valuation gate would have scored this low.
- Credit pillar: the Baa-10Y spread widened in late 1989 (~1.75% -> ~2.0%),
  retraced to ~1.5% by April 1990, then widened sharply after the invasion;
  it stood near 2.0% at the October equity trough and peaked at ~2.35% in
  December 1990-January 1991, after the equity low. Junk yields reached ~18%.
  Corporate debt/GDP at a record after the LBO decade. The standardized SLOOS
  series begins 1990Q2 - mid-episode - already showing broad net tightening,
  elevated into 1991.
- Liquidity/rates: real Fed funds ~4-5% through 1989; 10Y-2Y and 10Y-3M both
  inverted in 1989 and re-steepened into the recession - textbook sequence.
  M2 growth ran ~8-9% through 1986, slowed sharply during 1987 (~3.6% YoY by
  end-1987), and fluctuated in a roughly 3-6% range through 1988-90.
- Confirmation: CFNAI was choppy and intermittently negative through 1989 and
  early 1990, turning persistently negative from mid-1990 (monthly readings
  negative from July 1990 onward; CFNAI-MA3 negative from roughly Q2 1990) -
  note it is a backcast series and did not exist in real time in 1990. S&P
  broke its 200-DMA within days of the invasion; Sahm rule crossed 0.50 with
  the November 1990 reading (published early December), about two months after
  the equity trough - confirming the recession, not the equity low. VIX data
  begins Jan 1990: ~16-18 at the July equity peak (low 20s by the eve of the
  invasion) to ~36 in late August - confirmation only.
- Data limits: margin debt YoY (1997+), RSP/SPY breadth (2003+), and Fed
  balance sheet / net liquidity (post-2008 concepts) are all n/a here.

## Lessons for the monitor
The credit pillar plus curve inversion and restrictive real rates gave a
defensible stage-3/4 warning by early 1990; valuation contributed nothing.
The oil half of the decline was unforecastable - the monitor's job was to flag
the vulnerability the shock landed on, and the inputs to do so existed.
