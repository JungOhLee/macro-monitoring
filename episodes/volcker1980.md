# Volcker double-dip (peak: November 1980)

**Type: policy-induced bear.** The one crisis caused by the cure — proof that
the liquidity pillar can outvote cheap valuation and produce a 27% bear from
single-digit CAPE.

## The setup
A decade of stop-go policy and two oil shocks entrenched inflation: CPI hit
14.8% YoY in March 1980, gold touched $850 in January 1980, and energy stocks
were ~28% of S&P cap. Volcker took over the Fed in August 1979 and on
October 6, 1979 abandoned funds-rate targeting for money-supply (nonborrowed
reserve) targeting — deliberately letting rates go wherever killing inflation
required. The funds rate hit ~20% by spring 1980.

## The causal chain
Entrenched wage-price spiral -> October 1979 monetarist regime shift -> funds
rate to 20% + March 1980 credit controls -> sharp but brief 1980 recession ->
premature easing (funds rate to ~9% by July 1980) -> inflation and economy
rebound -> re-tightening to 20% by December 1980 -> real rates swing to +8-10%
-> second, deeper recession from July 1981 -> rate-sensitive sectors and
earnings collapse -> S&P -27% into August 1982.

## The turn
The S&P peaked November 28, 1980 at 140.52, just as the second push to 20%
hit. Rate-sensitive real activity broke first — housing starts and autos
collapsed through 1981 — and the energy complex (the index's largest sector)
rolled over with oil. The 10Y yield peaked at ~15.8% in September 1981.
The S&P ground down 27% over 20 months to 102.42 on August 12, 1982.
Mexico's default that same week forced the pivot; Volcker was already cutting,
and the disinflation bull added ~37% by year-end 1982.

## What it looked like in the indicators
- Valuation pillar: cheap the entire way. CAPE was ~9.7 at the November 1980 peak and ~6.6 at the trough; Buffett indicator near secular lows. But ERP vs 14% Treasuries was thin — bonds competed with everything.
- Liquidity pillar: the signal. Real Fed funds swung from negative through the 1970s to +8-10% by 1981, the most restrictive on record; nominal M2 growth of ~9% masked deeply negative real money growth.
- Curves: 10Y-3M and 10Y-2Y inverted deeply twice (1979-80 and 1980-81), correctly preceding both recessions.
- Real economy: the Sahm rule (computed retrospectively) triggered in both downturns; CFNAI was deeply negative; unemployment rose 7.2% -> 10.8%. Baa spreads widened toward ~300bp but were coincident, not leading.
- Data limits: no VIX (1990+), no RSP/SPY breadth (2003+), margin debt and SLOOS outside monitor coverage for this era. The trade-weighted dollar's ~30% surge 1980-82 is the cleanest tight-money confirmation available.

## Lessons for the monitor
A valuation-anchored sequencer would have been long into a -27% bear. The
catchable signals were curve inversion + record-positive real rates + Sahm
trigger stacking — and the exit was equally rule-friendly: the real-rate peak
and Fed pivot in August 1982 marked the best entry in a generation.
